# planning.md — Provenance Guard

> Spec written **before** implementation (Milestone 2). Every number here is a contract the code
> must implement. Updated before any stretch feature is started.

Provenance Guard is a backend service a creative-sharing platform plugs in to estimate whether
submitted text was written by a human or generated by AI, express that estimate **with honest
uncertainty**, show readers a plain-language transparency label, and let creators appeal. The
guiding principle throughout: **a false "AI" verdict on a real author's work is the worst outcome**,
so the system is tuned to be slow to accuse and quick to say "uncertain."

---

## 1. Detection signals

The pipeline uses **two distinct signals** that measure genuinely different properties of the text
and fail in different directions. (A third signal is added later as a documented stretch feature;
see §8.)

### Signal 1 — LLM semantic classifier (Groq `llama-3.3-70b-versatile`)
- **Measures:** holistic semantic & stylistic coherence — voice consistency, lived specificity,
  natural irregularity vs. the smooth, hedged, evenly-balanced feel typical of model output.
- **Output:** `llm_score` ∈ [0, 1] = estimated probability the text is AI-generated, plus a short
  rationale string. The model is prompted to return strict JSON: `{"ai_probability": 0.0-1.0,
  "rationale": "..."}`. We parse and clamp it.
- **Why it differs human↔AI:** humans write idiosyncratically; models trend toward balanced, "safe"
  prose.
- **Blind spot:** mislabels *polished* human writing (academic / corporate / non-native-English
  formal register) as AI, and can be fooled by *deliberately roughened* AI. Non-deterministic and
  not fully auditable.
- **Offline note:** when `GROQ_API_KEY` is unset, a deterministic heuristic stand-in produces a
  comparable `llm_score` so the system is runnable and testable without network. The production
  path is the real Groq call. (Implementation choice finalized in M3.)

### Signal 2 — Stylometric heuristics (pure Python, no external libs)
- **Measures:** statistical *shape* of the text via three sub-metrics:
  1. **Sentence-length burstiness** — coefficient of variation (CV = stdev/mean) of sentence
     lengths in words. Humans mix short and long sentences (high CV); AI clusters near a mean (low
     CV).
  2. **Type-token ratio (TTR)** — unique words ÷ total words. Captures vocabulary diversity.
  3. **Punctuation diversity/density** — rate of "expressive" marks (— ; : … ! ? () and irregular
     casing) per sentence. Human prose tends to use more varied punctuation.
- **Output:** `stylometry_score` ∈ [0, 1] where **high = uniform / AI-like**. Computed as a
  weighted blend of the three sub-metrics (each first mapped to a 0–1 "AI-likeness"):
  `stylometry_score = 0.50·uniformity_sentences + 0.25·uniformity_ttr + 0.25·uniformity_punct`.
- **Why it differs human↔AI:** AI text is statistically *more uniform*; human text is *more
  variable*.
- **Blind spot:** blind to meaning. A repetitive, simple-vocabulary human poem looks "uniform" and
  scores AI-like (false positive); a long, varied, well-prompted AI essay can look "bursty" and
  score human-like (false negative). Needs enough text — a 2-sentence submission has no stable
  variance.

### Why this pairing
One signal is **semantic**, one is purely **structural**. They are independent, so their
*agreement* corroborates a verdict and — more importantly — their *disagreement* is the clearest
honest evidence of "we don't actually know," which is fed directly into the confidence score.

### How the signals combine
```
combined_p_ai = 0.65 · llm_score  +  0.35 · stylometry_score
```
The LLM is weighted higher because semantic reading is the more reliable signal; stylometry acts as
a corroborator and tie-breaker. Both live in [0, 1]; `combined_p_ai` is therefore in [0, 1] and is
the probability-of-AI used for the attribution verdict.

> **Note (final design):** the two-signal pairing above is the required core. The deployed pipeline
> additionally runs a third **lexical** signal as a documented weighted ensemble — see §8a. The
> confidence math in §2 generalizes to N signals by defining `agreement = 1 − spread` across the
> signal scores, which for two signals is identical to `1 − |a − b|`.

## 2. Uncertainty representation

Two separate numbers, because they answer different questions:

- **`combined_p_ai` ∈ [0,1]** — *which way* does the evidence lean (AI vs. human)?
- **`confidence` ∈ [0,1]** — *how sure* are we in that lean?

### Attribution thresholds (on `combined_p_ai`) — deliberately asymmetric
| `combined_p_ai` | Attribution |
| --- | --- |
| `≥ 0.70` | `likely_ai` |
| `0.40 – 0.6999` | `uncertain` |
| `< 0.40` | `likely_human` |

The "AI" cutoff is set high (0.70) with a **wide uncertain band (0.40–0.70)** beneath it. This is
the false-positive-aversion knob: it takes strong, agreeing evidence to call something AI, while
anything ambiguous defaults to "uncertain" rather than an accusation. So **0.6 means "leaning AI
but inside the uncertain band — not confident enough to say AI."**

### Confidence formula
```
extremity  = 2 · |combined_p_ai − 0.5|          # 0 at the 0.5 midpoint, 1 at the extremes
agreement  = 1 − |llm_score − stylometry_score| # 1 when both signals agree, →0 when they diverge
confidence = clamp( 0.6 · extremity + 0.4 · agreement , 0, 1 )
```
A verdict is only "confident" when it is **both** far from the middle **and** backed by two agreeing
signals. Worked examples:

| Case | llm | stylo | combined_p_ai | extremity | agreement | confidence |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Clearly AI | 0.90 | 0.85 | 0.88 | 0.76 | 0.95 | **0.84** |
| Clearly human | 0.10 | 0.15 | 0.12 | 0.76 | 0.95 | **0.84** |
| Borderline, signals split | 0.70 | 0.25 | 0.54 | 0.08 | 0.55 | **0.27** |
| Formal/ESL human (both lean AI, agree) | 0.60 | 0.65 | 0.62 | 0.24 | 0.95 | **0.52** |

### Label selection (verdict **and** confidence together)
The transparency label is **not** chosen by `combined_p_ai` alone — confidence gates it, so a
low-confidence "AI" lean never shows the accusatory label:

```
if attribution == "uncertain":                  -> UNCERTAIN label
elif attribution == "likely_ai"   and confidence >= 0.70:  -> HIGH-CONFIDENCE AI label
elif attribution == "likely_human" and confidence >= 0.70: -> HIGH-CONFIDENCE HUMAN label
else:                                            -> UNCERTAIN label   # verdict exists but we're not sure enough
```
Result: the label visibly changes with confidence (not just the number), and the only path to the
"Likely AI-generated" label is a high `combined_p_ai` **with** agreeing signals.

## 3. Transparency label design — three variants (verbatim)

Plain language, no jargon, false-positive-aware. Each label has a `headline`, a `body`, and a
`level` string. The exact text:

### Variant A — High-confidence AI (`level: "High confidence"`)
> **Likely AI-generated**
>
> Our automated checks strongly indicate this text was generated by AI. Two independent checks — one
> that reads the writing's style and voice, and one that measures its structure and rhythm — both
> pointed the same way. This is a confident estimate, not proof. If you're the creator and you wrote
> this yourself, you can dispute it and a person will review it.

### Variant B — High-confidence human (`level: "High confidence"`)
> **Likely human-written**
>
> Our automated checks strongly indicate a person wrote this text. Both its voice and its structure
> look characteristic of human writing. This is a confident estimate, not a guarantee.

### Variant C — Uncertain (`level: "Low confidence — inconclusive"`)
> **Attribution uncertain**
>
> We can't tell with confidence whether this text was written by a person or generated by AI — our
> checks either disagreed or landed in the middle. AI detection is imperfect, and formal, technical,
> or non-native-English writing is easy to misread as AI. We'd rather flag the uncertainty than
> guess. If you're the creator, you can add context through an appeal.

These three are the only variants returned. A low-confidence AI/human *lean* deliberately collapses
to Variant C so a reader is never shown a confident accusation the system can't stand behind.

## 4. Appeals workflow

- **Who can appeal:** the creator of a submission, referencing the `content_id` they received. (This
  prototype does not enforce hard auth on `creator_id`; a real deployment would verify identity.
  Noted as a known simplification.)
- **What they provide:** `{ content_id, creator_reasoning }` — free-text reasoning for the dispute.
- **What the system does on receipt:**
  1. Look up the original decision by `content_id`. If none → `404 not found`.
  2. Update that content record's `status`: `classified` → `under_review`.
  3. Append a **new** audit-log entry of type `appeal`, carrying `creator_reasoning` plus the
     original attribution, confidence, both signal scores, and a timestamp — so the appeal sits
     **alongside** the original classification in the log.
  4. Return `{ content_id, status: "under_review", message }`.
  - No automated re-classification (per spec).
- **What a human reviewer sees:** the appeal queue is every record with `status == under_review`.
  For each, the reviewer sees the original text reference, `llm_score`, `stylometry_score`,
  `combined_p_ai`, `confidence`, the label that was shown, and the creator's reasoning, side by
  side, so they can make a human judgment.

## 5. Anticipated edge cases (where the system handles content poorly)

1. **Repetitive, simple-vocabulary poetry.** A human poem built on refrains, short even-length
   lines, and plain words produces **low sentence-length variance** and **low TTR** → `stylometry_
   score` spikes toward AI-like. This is a structural false positive. *Mitigation:* stylometry is
   only 35% of the blend, the uncertain band is wide, and the appeal path exists — but this content
   genuinely sits near the boundary and is called out in the README's Known Limitations.
2. **Very short submissions (≤ ~3 sentences).** Burstiness (CV) is statistically meaningless with so
   few sentences, so `stylometry_score` is noisy and the verdict leans almost entirely on the LLM.
   *Mitigation:* below a minimum length the system lowers confidence and biases toward the uncertain
   label rather than pretending the structural signal is informative.
3. **Lightly-edited / hybrid AI text** (human edits over an AI draft). The two signals tend to
   **split** (one reads human voice, the other still sees uniform structure), producing low
   `agreement` → low `confidence` → the uncertain label. This is the *intended* honest behavior, but
   it means hybrids are rarely given a firm verdict.

## Architecture

Provenance Guard is a small Flask service. **Submission flow:** `POST /submit` takes raw text
through the rate limiter, runs the LLM semantic signal and the stylometric signal in parallel, fuses
them into `combined_p_ai` and a `confidence` value (which drops when the signals disagree), maps
those to an attribution verdict and one of three plain-language transparency labels, writes a
structured audit-log entry, and returns the result. **Appeal flow:** `POST /appeal` looks up a prior
decision by `content_id`, flips its status to `under_review`, logs the appeal beside the original
entry, and returns a confirmation for a human reviewer to pick up.

### Submission flow
```
                          ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  creator's text          │              PROVENANCE GUARD               │
  { text, creator_id }    │                                             │
        │                 │   ┌───────────────┐                         │
        ▼                 │   │ rate limiter  │  (10/min, 100/day)       │
  POST /submit ───────────┼──▶│  Flask-Limiter│──429 if exceeded──┐      │
        │  raw text       │   └───────┬───────┘                   │      │
        │                 │           │ raw text                  │      │
        │                 │           ▼                           │      │
        │                 │   ┌───────────────┐  ┌──────────────┐ │      │
        │                 │   │ Signal 1: LLM │  │ Signal 2:    │ │      │
        │                 │   │ semantic(Groq)│  │ stylometry   │ │      │
        │                 │   └───────┬───────┘  └──────┬───────┘ │      │
        │                 │  llm_score│ (0-1)    stylo_ │(0-1)    │      │
        │                 │           ▼                 ▼         │      │
        │                 │      ┌─────────────────────────────┐  │      │
        │                 │      │ confidence scoring / fusion │  │      │
        │                 │      │ combined_p_ai + confidence  │  │      │
        │                 │      │ (+ signal-agreement check)  │  │      │
        │                 │      └──────────────┬──────────────┘  │      │
        │                 │       combined_p_ai │ + confidence    │      │
        │                 │                     ▼                 │      │
        │                 │      ┌─────────────────────────────┐  │      │
        │                 │      │ attribution + label select  │  │      │
        │                 │      │ likely_ai / uncertain /     │  │      │
        │                 │      │ likely_human  → label text  │  │      │
        │                 │      └──────────────┬──────────────┘  │      │
        │                 │        label + all  │ scores          │      │
        │                 │                     ▼                 │      │

```

### Appeal flow
```
  creator (has content_id)
  { content_id, creator_reasoning }
        │
        ▼
  POST /appeal ──▶ look up original decision by content_id
        │                 │
        │                 ▼
        │          update status: classified ──▶ "under_review"
        │                 │
        │                 ▼
        │          audit log: NEW entry beside original
        │          (carries creator_reasoning + original scores)
        │                 │
        ▼                 ▼
  { content_id, status:"under_review", message }   ──▶ human-review queue
                                                       (= all under_review)
```

### API surface
| Method & path | Accepts | Returns |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `POST /submit` | `{ text, creator_id }` | `{ content_id, attribution, confidence, combined_p_ai, signals:{ llm_score, stylometry_score }, label:{ headline, body, level } }` |
| `POST /appeal` | `{ content_id, creator_reasoning }` | `{ content_id, status:"under_review", message }` |
| `GET /log` | `?limit=` | `{ entries:[ …structured records… ] }` |
| `GET /health` | — | `{ status:"ok" }` |

**Storage:** in-memory dict `content_id → record` for fast lookup, plus an append-only structured
audit log persisted to `audit_log.json` (list of JSON objects) and surfaced via `GET /log`.

## 6. Rate limiting (decided here, implemented in M5)
- **Chosen limits:** `10 per minute` and `100 per day` per client IP, on `POST /submit`.
- **Reasoning:** a real creator submits their own work occasionally — a handful of pieces in a
  sitting at most. 10/min comfortably covers a human revising and resubmitting, while stopping a
  script from hammering the (cost-bearing) LLM signal; 100/day caps sustained abuse and runaway LLM
  spend from a single source. Read endpoints (`/log`, `/health`) are unlimited.

## 7. Audit-log record shape (decided here)
```json
{
  "type": "classification",
  "content_id": "uuid",
  "creator_id": "test-user-1",
  "timestamp": "2026-06-29T14:32:10.123Z",
  "attribution": "likely_ai",
  "combined_p_ai": 0.88,
  "confidence": 0.84,
  "llm_score": 0.90,
  "stylometry_score": 0.85,
  "label_headline": "Likely AI-generated",
  "status": "classified"
}
```
Appeal entries use `"type": "appeal"`, add `"creator_reasoning"`, and set `"status":
"under_review"`, repeating the original scores so the dispute and decision read together.

## 8. Stretch plan (specced here before building, per the milestone rules)

Targeting the easy, code-only bonuses.

### 8a. Ensemble detection (built)
Add a third, genuinely distinct signal and fuse all three as a documented weighted ensemble:
- **Signal 3 — lexical fingerprint:** surface-level cues — density of AI cliché/buzzword phrases
  ("paradigm", "leverage", "stakeholders", "in today's digital landscape", …) vs. casual human
  markers and slang ("ok so", "honestly", "kinda", "…"). Output `lexical_score` ∈ [0,1], high =
  AI-like. This is distinct from Signal 1 (which now reads *register/voice*, not surface phrases)
  and Signal 2 (structure).
- **Ensemble weights (sum to 1):** semantic `0.55`, structural `0.25`, lexical `0.20`. The semantic
  signal is the most reliable so it carries the most weight; lexical is the noisiest so it carries
  the least.
- **Conflict resolution:** the weighted mean sets `combined_p_ai`. Disagreement is measured as the
  **spread** (max − min) across the three signal scores; `agreement = 1 − spread`, which feeds the
  same confidence formula from §2 (for two signals this reduces exactly to `1 − |a − b|`). When the
  signals conflict, confidence falls, and a verdict that fails the confidence gate is shown as
  "uncertain". Individual signal scores are returned in the `/submit` response and written to the
  audit log so the ensemble is fully transparent.

### 8b. Analytics dashboard (built)
`GET /analytics` computes, from the audit log: the **detection pattern** (counts + ratios of
likely_ai / uncertain / likely_human), the **appeal rate** (appeals ÷ classifications), and two more
metrics — **average confidence** and the **low-confidence share** (fraction shown "uncertain" on the
confidence gate).

### 8c. Provenance certificate (not built this pass)
Design sketch only: a "verified human" credential a creator earns via an out-of-band attestation
step, surfaced as a distinct badge above the transparency label. Deferred.
